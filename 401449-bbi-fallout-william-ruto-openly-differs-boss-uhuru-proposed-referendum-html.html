    BBI fallout: William Ruto openly differs with his boss Uhuru over proposed referendum

    Deputy President William Ruto has strongly differed with his boss President Uhuru Kenyatta over his position that the Building Bridges Initiative (BBI) referendum will better the lives of Kenyans by increasing budget allocations to counties.

    Ruto, who was addressing mourners at a funeral in Matungu, Kakamega county, on Saturday, January 30, described the BBI proposal to raise allocations to devolved units from 15% to 35% as an overambitious recommendation probably being used to woo governors and citizens into supporting the document.

    While taking a swipe at the proposal, the second in command said the government was having difficulty wiring 15% to counties and argued the 35% allocation would not be possible.

    "They are talking about raising the amount of money sent to counties to 35% of the national government's budget. How will this be possible if the current 15% cant be sent...unless they are planning to perform some magic or witcraft," said Ruto.

    Last week, the Council of Governor (COG) decried delayed disbursement of funds from the Treasury. According to CoG, most of the devolved units had not received their December and January allocations.

    The DP has also, on several occasions, described the BBI as a document seeking to reward some powerful individuals in government with plum positions such as president, prime minister and deputy prime minister.

    As Ruto continued to poke holes into the BBI, Uhuru was at Sagana State lodge in Nyeri county where he met over 7,000 delegates and drummed support for the BBI.

    The president advised Kenyans to be wary of politicians opposed to BBI process saying they were not being sincere in their opposition to the popular initiative.

    “BBI is about allocating more resources to the grassroots. Kenyans should not be fooled by a section of politicians opposed to the document to further their selfish interests at the expense of the common good.For instance, is it wrong to have more resources allocated to counties? And if it is not wrong, why are they opposing the BBI document,” Uhuru posed.

    Uhuru's meeting with delegates from Mt Kenya comes against the backdrop of speculation that the document, which is a product of the president's handshake with ODM leader Raila Odinga, was not popular in the region.

    The fears were first raised by Senate Majority Whip Irung'u Kang'ata about a month ago in a controversial letter to Uhuru.

    Jubilee Party's vice-chair David Murathe also recently raised a red flag that the document was not being popularised by Mt Kenya leaders.

    In a separate story, TUKO.co.ke reported that about 41 lawmakers from Mt Kenya had written to the president that they were finding it difficult to market Raila in the region.
