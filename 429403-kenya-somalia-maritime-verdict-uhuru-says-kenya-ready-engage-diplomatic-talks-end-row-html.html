    President Uhuru Kenyatta has vowed to defend Kenya's territory following the ruling on the Kenya-Somalia maritime case by the International Court of Justice (ICJ).

    Uhuru said when he became president on April 9, 2013, he took an oath to protect the territorial integrity of the Republic of Kenya.

    Protecting the nation against foreign aggression

    He also urged other nations to respect Kenya's efforts aimed at protecting its territories from foreign aggression.

    ''I will do everything possible as President and Commander-in-Chief, to preserve the territory of this our great Republic. We beseech the rest of the family of Nations to appreciate and respect our inherent right to protect, by all means, our territory,'' Uhuru said.

    According to a statement from State House, Kenya, seen by TUKO.co.ke, the head of state observed that Kenya is not surprised at the decision of the court involving the Maritime Delimitation in the Indian Ocean Case (Somalia Kenya).

    ''However, Kenya is profoundly concerned by the import of the decision and its implications for the Horn of Africa region, and international law generally. At the outset, Kenya wishes to indicate that it rejects in totality and does not recognize the findings in the decision,'' Uhuru explained.

    Uhuru indicated that the court's decision on the matter embodies its perpetuation of jurisdictional overreach and raises a fundamental question on the respect of the sovereignty and consent of States to international judicial processes.

    ''International tribunals have jurisdiction ONLY to the extent of CONSENT by a State. Kenya, as an avid supporter of the rule of law, accepted the Courtâ€™s jurisdiction through a declaration in 1965, with an objectively clear outline of certain excluded matters. At the time, Kenya never imagined that the ICJ would violate the declaration to the extent of,'' he said.

    The head of state called on the international community to create an enabling environment to pursue a negotiated settlement.

    ''Kenya and Somalia are neighboring States, with a common border, and communities with shared social, cultural, and religious practices. This decision is, in the circumstances, a zero-sum game, which will strain the relations between the two countries,'' he said.

    The maritime row

    As earlier reported by TUKO.co.ke, Since 2014, the two neighboring countries have contested over which direction their border extends into the Indian Ocean.

    In a ruling made on Tuesday, October 12, the Hague-based court dismissed Kenya's claim to Somalia's similar latitude (horizontal) boundary.

    As a result, the ICJ ruled that there is no de facto maritime border between the two countries, setting a new border that cuts through the disputed area (100,000 square kilometres).

    However, Uhuru said that Kenya is committed to a diplomatic solution to the current impasse.

    He reiterated that his administration would resolve the matter through the institutions of the African Union, such as the African Union Border Programme and its Peace and Security architecture, in addition to other bilateral arrangements.
